ISLINGTON AVE URBAN REDESIGN

EXPLORING A KEY TORONTO URBAN CORRIDOR AND THE ROLE OF DESIGN IN SHAPING PUBLIC LIFE

This project examines Islington Avenue near the Bloor-Islington intersection in Toronto, focusing on its architectural and urban design elements and its impact on public life, street density, and spatial justice. The study highlights how urban design can shape social equity while increasing densification near a key public transportation hub.

Key aspects of the study include evaluating walkability, the quality and extent of the tree canopy, sidewalk accessibility, and active versus passive street edges. These elements are assessed to determine their impact on social engagement, environmental comfort, and overall street vitality.

The project highlights disparities in spatial justice while proposing design strategies to enhance connectivity and prioritize human-scale urban development. Techniques such as mapping and diagramming are used to articulate current and proposed patterns in accessibility, infrastructure, and the potential of public spaces.

This project reflects a broader interest in how urban design can promote spatial justice while addressing the challenges of densification, ensuring architectural and public interventions contribute to a more sustainable, inclusive urban future.

Key recommendations include:

  • Expanding green infrastructure to create a more comfortable and sustainable environment.
  • Activating passive street edges with mixed-use development and public-facing amenities to enhance street-level engagement.
  • Concentrating density and urban design improvements around the transit hub to maximize accessibility and connectivity.
  • Implementing pedestrian-friendly design interventions to prioritize public life.
